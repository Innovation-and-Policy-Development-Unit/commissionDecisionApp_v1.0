AI FEATURES — COMMISSION DECISION PORTAL & CASE MANAGEMENT SYSTEM

CATEGORY A — SUBMISSION INTELLIGENCE (CDP)
-------------------------------------------

A1. AUTO-FILL COMPLIANCE CHECKLIST
   What it does:
     When an HR Manager uploads a submission (e.g. a Notice of Disciplinary Action)
     along with supporting documents, Claude reads the submission text and any
     attached PDFs, then automatically pre-fills the compliance checklist fields
     (e.g. "Was the employee given 48-hour notice?", "Is the allegation specific?",
     "Has the employee's supervisor signed?"). The officer reviews and confirms or
     adjusts the AI-filled answers rather than reading every document from scratch.
   Why it matters:
     Checklist completion is the most time-consuming manual step. Pre-filling it
     reduces officer workload by an estimated 60–70% per submission.
   Model recommended: Claude Haiku 4.5 (fast, cheap for routine extraction)
   Effort: Medium | Impact: High

A2. DOCUMENT CLASSIFICATION
   What it does:
     When a document is uploaded (PDF, image, Word), Claude identifies what type
     of document it is — e.g. "Appointment Letter", "Notice of Allegation",
     "Medical Certificate", "Employment Contract", "PSC Form 7" — and tags it
     automatically in the system. Officers no longer need to manually label each file.
   Why it matters:
     Submissions often arrive with 5–15 attachments. Mis-categorised documents
     cause delays when compliance officers search for specific records later.
   Model recommended: Claude Haiku 4.5
   Effort: Low | Impact: Medium

A3. MISSING INFORMATION DETECTOR
   What it does:
     Before an HR Manager can submit, Claude scans the draft submission for
     required fields and documents. It produces a plain-English list of what is
     missing or incomplete — e.g. "Employee's response to the allegation is not
     attached", "Date of incident is not specified", "Supervisor signature is absent".
     The system blocks submission until critical gaps are resolved.
   Why it matters:
     A large proportion of submissions are returned to ministries due to missing
     information, adding weeks to the process. This feature stops incomplete
     submissions at source.
   Model recommended: Claude Haiku 4.5
   Effort: Low | Impact: High

A4. DUPLICATE / SIMILAR SUBMISSION DETECTOR
   What it does:
     When a new submission is received, Claude compares it against existing cases
     in the system (same subject name, ministry, allegation type, date range) and
     flags if a highly similar or duplicate case already exists. Officers are shown
     a warning with a link to the existing case.
   Why it matters:
     Prevents duplicate case creation when a ministry accidentally submits twice
     or when a case is re-submitted under a slightly different subject name.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6 (needs more reasoning for similarity matching)
   Effort: Medium | Impact: Medium

A5. SUBMISSION QUALITY SCORE
   What it does:
     After submission, Claude assigns a quality score (e.g. 78/100) based on
     completeness, clarity of the allegation, quality of supporting evidence, and
     adherence to PSC formatting requirements. The score and a brief explanation
     are shown to the compliance officer as context when they open the case.
   Why it matters:
     Gives officers an immediate signal of how much work a submission needs
     before it is ready for the Commission to consider.
   Model recommended: Claude Haiku 4.5
   Effort: Low | Impact: Medium


CATEGORY B — CASE MANAGEMENT INTELLIGENCE (CMS)
-------------------------------------------------

B1. CASE SUMMARY GENERATOR
   What it does:
     At any point during a case, a compliance officer can click "Generate Summary"
     and Claude reads the full case file — submission text, uploaded documents,
     case notes, stage history, employee responses — and produces a structured
     1–2 page summary in plain English. The summary includes: background,
     allegation, timeline, employee response, key evidence, current stage, and
     outstanding actions.
   Why it matters:
     When a case transfers between officers, or when the Commission Secretary
     needs to brief a new reviewer, reading the full file can take hours. A
     one-click summary saves significant time.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6 (needs to synthesise complex information)
   Effort: Low | Impact: High

B2. RISK ASSESSMENT
   What it does:
     Claude analyses case attributes — seniority of the subject, nature of the
     allegation, prior cases involving the same person, ministry history, and SLA
     status — and produces a risk rating (Low / Medium / High / Critical) with a
     short explanation. High-risk cases are visually flagged on the case dashboard.
   Why it matters:
     Ensures that senior executive misconduct cases and politically sensitive
     matters are not buried in a queue and receive appropriate priority.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6
   Effort: Medium | Impact: High

B3. RECOMMENDED OUTCOME
   What it does:
     Based on the case facts, allegation type, evidence, and employee response,
     Claude suggests a recommended outcome (e.g. "Formal Warning", "Termination",
     "No Further Action") and provides a short rationale citing comparable case
     types. The officer retains full decision-making authority — this is advisory only.
   Why it matters:
     New or junior compliance officers benefit from a reference point, especially
     for case types they have not handled before. Reduces inconsistent outcomes
     across similar cases.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6 (requires careful reasoning)
   Effort: High | Impact: High
   Note: Must include a clear disclaimer that this is AI-generated guidance only.

B4. SLA BREACH PREDICTOR
   What it does:
     Claude monitors case progress and stage durations. When it predicts that a
     stage is likely to breach its statutory deadline — based on current pace,
     officer workload, and historical patterns — it sends an early warning
     notification to the assigned officer and their supervisor. Earlier than the
     standard "3 days to due date" system alert.
   Why it matters:
     Many SLA breaches are avoidable. Predictive warnings give officers time to
     act rather than just flagging a breach after it happens.
   Model recommended: Claude Haiku 4.5
   Effort: Medium | Impact: High

B5. AUTO-DRAFT NOTICE OF ALLEGATION
   What it does:
     From the case facts (subject details, allegation description, date of incident,
     regulatory reference), Claude drafts a formal Notice of Allegation letter in
     the standard PSC format. The officer reviews, edits, and approves before it
     is sent. The draft includes: addressee, formal allegation statement,
     response deadline, and the relevant PSC regulation citation.
   Why it matters:
     Drafting these letters from scratch takes 30–60 minutes per case. A good
     AI draft reduces this to a 5-minute review.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6 (formal government writing quality)
   Effort: Medium | Impact: High


CATEGORY C — REPORTING & ANALYTICS
-------------------------------------

C1. NATURAL LANGUAGE REPORT GENERATION
   What it does:
     A manager or the Commission Secretary types a plain-English question such as:
     "How many disciplinary cases were opened in the Ministry of Finance this year?"
     or "Show me all overdue cases involving senior executives" — and the system
     generates the report, a data table, and an optional chart. No SQL or manual
     filtering required.
   Why it matters:
     Current reporting requires technical staff or pre-built report templates.
     Natural language queries allow non-technical users to get answers instantly
     without waiting for IT.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6 (translates natural language to query logic)
   Effort: Medium | Impact: High

C2. AI-GENERATED EXECUTIVE SUMMARY FOR COMMISSION MEETINGS
   What it does:
     Before each Commission sitting, Claude compiles a structured executive
     briefing document: cases ready for decision, outstanding matters from the
     previous sitting, SLA performance summary, cases at risk, and any flagged
     high-priority matters. Produced automatically from live system data.
   Why it matters:
     Preparing Commission briefing packs is currently a manual half-day task.
     Automating the first draft significantly reduces administrative burden on
     the Secretary's office.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6
   Effort: Medium | Impact: High

C3. TREND ANALYSIS
   What it does:
     Claude analyses patterns across all cases over time and surfaces insights
     such as: which ministries have the highest misconduct rates, which case types
     are increasing, seasonal patterns in submissions, and whether outcome
     consistency has changed over quarters. Presented as a dashboard report.
   Why it matters:
     The Commission can use this data to make systemic recommendations to
     Government and target capacity-building support at high-risk ministries.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6
   Effort: Medium | Impact: Medium

C4. MEETING MINUTES → ACTION ITEMS EXTRACTOR
   What it does:
     An officer uploads or pastes meeting minutes text. Claude extracts:
     decisions made, action items (with owner and deadline if mentioned),
     matters deferred, and follow-up questions. The output is formatted as a
     structured action register that can be saved directly to the case or
     system record.
   Why it matters:
     Action items from Commission meetings often get buried in long minutes
     documents. Structured extraction ensures nothing is missed.
   Model recommended: Claude Haiku 4.5
   Effort: Low | Impact: Medium


CATEGORY D — CHATBOT / CONVERSATIONAL INTERFACE
-------------------------------------------------

D1. STAFF CHATBOT — PSC REGULATIONS & PROCEDURES
   What it does:
     A chat interface available to all system users where staff can ask questions
     about PSC Act provisions, public service regulations, and internal procedures
     in plain English. For example: "What is the maximum period of temporary
     suspension without pay?", "What are the steps for a grievance process?",
     "Which form do I use for a serious misconduct referral?". Claude answers from
     the PSC Act, regulations, and procedure manuals loaded as its knowledge base.
   Why it matters:
     HR officers in ministries frequently delay submissions because they are
     unsure of the correct procedure. A reliable, instant Q&A tool reduces
     procedural errors and speeds up the compliance process.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6 with RAG (document retrieval)
   Effort: Medium | Impact: High

D2. SUBMISSION STATUS CHATBOT
   What it does:
     An employee or HR manager can type "What is the status of case PSC-2026-00042?"
     or "Has my submission been reviewed yet?" and receive a plain-English status
     update without having to navigate the portal or call the PSC office.
   Why it matters:
     Status enquiries are one of the highest-volume administrative tasks for
     the PSC Secretary's office. A self-service chatbot frees up staff time.
   Model recommended: Claude Haiku 4.5
   Effort: Medium | Impact: High

D3. COMPLIANCE OFFICER CASE ASSISTANT
   What it does:
     A case-specific chatbot available to the assigned compliance officer. The
     officer can ask questions about their open case: "What does PSC Reg 28 say
     about this type of allegation?", "Draft a response to the employee's latest
     submission", "Summarise what happened in Stage 2". Claude has access to the
     full case file as context.
   Why it matters:
     Reduces the need for officers to search regulations manually or consult
     seniors for every procedural question during case management.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6 (needs deep case context)
   Effort: High | Impact: High


CATEGORY E — DOCUMENT PROCESSING
-----------------------------------

E1. OCR + KEY FACTS EXTRACTION FROM SCANNED DOCUMENTS
   What it does:
     When a scanned image or non-searchable PDF is uploaded (e.g. a handwritten
     letter, a scanned appointment letter), Claude extracts and structures the
     key facts: names, dates, positions, references, and relevant statements.
     The extracted text is stored as searchable metadata alongside the file.
   Why it matters:
     Many supporting documents in PSC cases are scanned paper records. Without
     extraction, these are unsearchable and must be read manually every time.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6 (vision capability for image inputs)
   Effort: Medium | Impact: Medium

E2. MEETING MINUTES → STRUCTURED RECORD
   What it does:
     Upload or paste meeting minutes (Commission sitting, compliance review meeting,
     disciplinary hearing transcript) and Claude produces a structured record with:
     date, location, attendees, agenda items, decisions per item, action items
     with owners and deadlines, and matters deferred to next sitting.
   Why it matters:
     Meeting records are currently typed manually in full. Structured extraction
     from raw minutes saves hours per meeting and ensures consistent formatting.
   Model recommended: Claude Haiku 4.5
   Effort: Low | Impact: High

E3. AUTO-REDACT SENSITIVE DATA
   What it does:
     Before a document is shared externally (e.g. sent to a subject's ministry,
     exported for an audit), Claude identifies and redacts sensitive personal
     information — names of witnesses, personal addresses, medical details,
     third-party identifiers — that should not be disclosed. A redacted PDF is
     generated alongside the original.
   Why it matters:
     Manual redaction is error-prone and time-consuming. Accidental disclosure
     of sensitive information creates legal and reputational risk for the PSC.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6
   Effort: Medium | Impact: Medium


CATEGORY F — WORKFLOW AUTOMATION
-----------------------------------

F1. SMART CASE ROUTING
   What it does:
     When a new case is created and assigned to the compliance unit, Claude
     suggests which officer to assign based on: current workload (number of open
     cases), case type expertise, seniority of the subject (senior executive
     cases go to senior officers), and geographic/ministry familiarity.
   Why it matters:
     Manual assignment by a supervisor can be slow and inconsistent. Smart routing
     ensures balanced workloads and appropriate expertise matches.
   Model recommended: Claude Haiku 4.5
   Effort: Low | Impact: Medium

F2. DEADLINE NOTIFICATION DRAFTING
   What it does:
     When a case stage is nearing its deadline, the system auto-drafts a
     personalised reminder email to the responsible officer or ministry contact.
     The draft includes the case reference, what is outstanding, the exact
     deadline, and the consequence of non-compliance. Officers review and send.
   Why it matters:
     Generic automated reminders are often ignored. A specific, well-written
     reminder drafted with full case context is more likely to prompt action.
   Model recommended: Claude Haiku 4.5
   Effort: Low | Impact: Medium

F3. COMPLIANCE OUTCOME LETTER GENERATOR
   What it does:
     After the Commission reaches a decision, Claude drafts the formal outcome
     letter to the subject employee and their ministry. The letter is in official
     PSC format and references the applicable regulations, the decision, and any
     required follow-up actions (e.g. reinstatement date, warning conditions).
   Why it matters:
     Outcome letters must be precise and legally worded. Drafting from scratch is
     slow and inconsistency in wording creates disputes. AI drafts ensure
     consistency and reduce drafting time significantly.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6 (formal legal/government writing)
   Effort: Medium | Impact: High

F4. POLICY Q&A FROM PSC ACT / REGULATIONS TEXT
   What it does:
     The full text of the PSC Act, Public Service Regulations, and relevant
     government circulars are loaded into the system as a document knowledge base.
     Any user — officer, HR manager, or administrator — can ask a question and
     receive a direct answer with the exact regulation section cited as a reference.
     For example: "What is the statutory period for a disciplinary suspension?"
     → "Under PSC Regulation 31(2), the maximum period is 3 months..."
   Why it matters:
     Regulation lookups currently require staff to manually search through PDF
     documents. Instant, cited answers reduce procedural errors and save time
     across every ministry using the system.
   Model recommended: Claude Sonnet 4.6 with RAG
   Effort: Medium | Impact: High
